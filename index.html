How UX Design Changed the Way I See Everyday Products

Before I started studying UX design, I used everyday products without thinking much about how they were made or why they worked the way they did. I would tap buttons, open apps, use appliances, and move on with my day. I never stopped considering that someone had to design every small detail I interacted with. But once I began learning UX, something shifted. Now I can’t look at anything the same way. Everything feels like a design decision waiting to be analyzed.

One of the first things I noticed was how many everyday products are confusing for no reason. For example, smartphones. I used to tap around without thinking, but now I notice how certain icons, gestures, and menu placements can make simple tasks harder than they need to be. Sometimes a setting is buried in three layers for no clear reason, or a button that should be obvious is tiny and easy to miss. It makes me think about how many people struggle with basic interactions because the interface wasn’t designed with real users in mind.

I also started paying attention to the design clues that show how something should be used. Door handles are big ones. Before UX, I never noticed how many doors have handles that look like you should pull them, even though you’re supposed to push. Now every time I see one, I immediately think, “This is a bad design. The handle is giving the wrong signal.” It’s funny how something so small can suddenly become so obvious once you learn the basics of UX.

Another big change is how I react to apps. I used to just accept whatever layout an app had. Now I catch myself noticing spacing, alignment and even the button placement. When an app makes something easy, I appreciate it more because I understand the work behind it. When an app makes something confusing, I immediately think about how I would redesign it. It’s like having a new lens that I can’t turn off.

Studying UX also made me more aware of accessibility. I never used to think about font sizes, color contrast, or whether a button was easy to tap. Now I notice when text is too small or when a website uses colors that are hard to read. It makes me realize how many people are excluded from experiences because accessibility wasn’t considered. This part of UX has changed me the most because it made me more empathetic toward different types of users.

Overall, learning UX design has made me more observant, more curious, and honestly a little more critical. But in a good way. I appreciate good design more deeply, and I notice bad design more quickly. UX has changed the way I move through the world, and I don’t think I’ll ever go back to seeing things the old way.
